The Stakes

Why the Packet Matters More Than You Think

A submittal packet is a recruiter's credibility on paper — sent to a hiring manager who will never meet the candidate before deciding whether to interview them.

01

One Error, One Lost Submission

A mismatched certification number or an inconsistent start date is often enough for a facility to reject a candidate outright — no questions asked, no second chance.

02

Compliance Has Deadlines

Licenses and certifications expire. A packet submitted with an expired credential — or one that will expire mid-contract — can disqualify a candidate before day one.

03

You Are the Last Line of Defense

The candidate doesn't proofread their own packet. The facility doesn't catch errors until it's too late. That review responsibility sits with you, the recruiter.

Think of it this way: the packet you submit today is the only version of your candidate the hiring manager will see until the interview. If it's sloppy, inconsistent, or incomplete, that becomes the candidate's reputation — even though it's really a reflection of the packet, not the person.
Document Anatomy

What's Inside a Submittal Packet

Most healthcare staffing packets are built from the same core documents, regardless of specialty. Here's what each one is, and why a facility requires it.

1

Submission Cover Sheet

The quick-reference summary a hiring manager scans first: contact info, availability, shift preference, certifications, and a snapshot of relevant experience.

Why it's required: it lets a busy hiring manager decide in 60 seconds whether to read further — accuracy here determines whether the rest of the packet even gets opened.
FIRST IMPRESSIONAVAILABILITYQUICK SCAN
2

Compliance / Clearance Screening Forms

Facility-specific or contract-specific screening questions — for example, a clearance form may ask whether the candidate has worked at a particular facility type, and if so, under what terms they left.

Why it's required: certain facilities and federal contracts carry legal or security obligations that go beyond standard credentialing — these forms document that the agency screened for them.
CLIENT-SPECIFICCOMPLIANCE
3

Resume / Candidate Profile

The full work history, skills summary, EMR/charting systems used, and detailed chronological experience — usually far more detailed than the cover sheet.

Why it's required: hiring managers need to verify specialty depth, recency of experience, and whether the candidate's background actually matches the unit they're hiring for.
WORK HISTORYSKILLS MATCH
4

Certifications & Licenses

Primary-source or candidate-provided proof of credentials — BLS/CPR cards, state licenses, specialty certifications — each with an issue date, expiration date, and unique certification number.

Why it's required: facilities are legally required to verify that anyone touching a patient holds active, unexpired credentials appropriate to their role.
LEGAL REQUIREMENTEXPIRATION-SENSITIVE
5

Government-Issued ID

A driver's license or equivalent, confirming legal name, date of birth, and address match what's listed elsewhere in the packet.

Why it's required: it's the anchor document — every other name, DOB, and address in the packet should trace back to this one without contradiction.
IDENTITY ANCHOR
6

Reference Checks & Self-Assessments

Structured evaluations from former supervisors covering communication, professionalism, dependability, and overall performance, plus candidate-completed skills self-assessments.

Why it's required: certifications prove a candidate can do the work in theory; references and self-assessments are the closest thing to proof they've done it well in practice.

Before You Hit Submit

Your Pre-Submission Checklist

Run through this every single time, regardless of how confident you feel about the packet.

ID

Identity Consistency

Name, date of birth, and address match exactly across the cover sheet, resume, ID, and any certifications. Middle names and suffixes are either present everywhere or consistently omitted.

#

Credential Numbers Match

Every certification or license number on the cover sheet/resume is the exact same number shown on the actual certificate image — not just the same expiration date.

DT

Dates Are Internally Consistent

Start date, availability date, and "requested time off" don't contradict each other. Certification issue and expiration dates match what's printed on the certificate itself.

YR

Experience Claims Add Up

"Years of experience" stated in a summary is mathematically consistent with the candidate's date of birth, education completion date, and earliest job listed in the work history.

Q&A

Forms Are Logically Complete

Conditional questions are answered consistently — if a screening form's first answer is "No," dependent follow-up fields further down should be blank or marked N/A, not filled in anyway.

REF

References Are Verifiable

Reference contact details are real working phone numbers/emails, and the reference's title and relationship to the candidate are stated clearly enough that a hiring manager could call to confirm.

Golden rule: never read a packet document by document. Read it field by field, cross-referencing the same piece of information (a name, a date, a number) everywhere it appears. That's where almost every real error hides.
